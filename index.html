Experience

I was first exposed to the language of python as a freshman in the Upsilon Lab where I was a member of the "Decoding Brain Signals" project which used tensorflow and pandas to analyse brain signals. Since then, I have interned at Caltech as a Summer Research Fellow (2021) and more recently at NASA-JPL as a Summer Intern (2022).

During my time with Caltech, I used the NIRSPEC data reduction pipeline and several python libraries to analyze the atmosphere of transiting exoplanet WASP-44b. By comparing the spectra during transit and out-of-transit, wavelength dependent changes in the amount of light blocked by the planet can be detected. We found that after data analysis that there is no significant increase in the transit depth at the 10830 Angstrom line. The metastable helium absorption line at 10830 Angstroms and is useful for probing outflows as it only exists in low-density regions of the atmosphere. You can read more about this project here. I presented my project results at the Caltech SURF commencement in 2021 and the UCLA Undergraduate Research week in 2022.

At NASA-JPL, I worked with Dr. Marie Ygouf, Dr. Rob Zellem, Dr. Vanessa Bailey and the Roman CGI Project Science Team to develop post-processing tools in python and to perform tests on the most recent Roman CGI simulated data. My past experience has taught me that physics and computer science are two complimentary fields. The former provides an analytical framework about the natural world while the latter applies practical insights to data science.

Relevant Course Work

Through my course work at UCLA, I have been exposed to the fundamentals of statistics and engineering. My statistical mechanics and astrophysics class (ASTRO 115), taught me how to apply "classical and quantum mechanics to large systems in order to derive the macroscopic behavior of matter". Statistics is a useful framework since astrophysical environments are large systems described only by statistical behavior.

My Analytic Mechanics class (PHYSICS 105B) taught me about Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formalism, Lorentz scalars, and theory of special relativity. We applied these concepts to rocket motion and wave oscillations. Other relevant courses include Mathematical Methods of Physics, Electrostatics/dynamics and Magnetostatics/dynamics, Thermodynamics, and Elements of Quantum Mechanics and Statistical Mechanics.
